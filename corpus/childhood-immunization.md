# Childhood Immunization Schedule (EPI)

## Section 1 — Definitions

- **EPI (Expanded Programme on Immunization)**: the national programme delivering routine childhood vaccines per the WHO-recommended schedule, adapted to country-specific epidemiology.
- **Antigen**: a single vaccine target (e.g., measles, polio, diphtheria).
- **Combination vaccine**: a single injection containing multiple antigens (e.g., pentavalent = DTP + hepatitis B + Hib).
- **Catch-up immunization**: providing missed vaccines to children who fell behind the schedule. The general principle: never restart a series — continue from where the child left off.
- **Fully immunized**: a child who has received all WHO-recommended antigens by their first birthday (the standard definition for coverage indicators).

## Section 2 — Roles and responsibilities

- **CHW**: identifies under-immunized children in the community via home visits and growth-monitoring sessions; conducts defaulter tracing for missed immunizations; counsels caregivers on the schedule.
- **Facility nurse**: administers vaccines per the schedule; cold-chain management; documents on the child's growth card and EPI register.
- **District EPI coordinator**: vaccine supply, cold-chain monitoring, outbreak response (e.g., supplementary measles campaigns).

CHWs do not administer vaccines. They support identification, scheduling, and follow-up.

## Section 3 — Standard schedule (Lesotho-aligned WHO recommendation)

This is the typical schedule in SSA programmes including Lesotho. Confirm exact local schedule with the District EPI Coordinator — schedules update periodically.

| Age | Antigens administered |
|---|---|
| At birth | BCG (tuberculosis), OPV0 (oral polio), Hep B birth dose |
| 6 weeks | Pentavalent 1 (DTP + HepB + Hib), OPV1, PCV1 (pneumococcal), Rotavirus 1 |
| 10 weeks | Pentavalent 2, OPV2, PCV2, Rotavirus 2 |
| 14 weeks | Pentavalent 3, OPV3, PCV3, IPV (inactivated polio), Rotavirus 3 (where given) |
| 9 months | Measles 1 (or MR — measles-rubella), Yellow Fever (where endemic — confirm Lesotho status) |
| 18 months | Measles 2 (or MR 2), DPT booster |
| School-age (9–14, girls) | HPV (where introduced) |

**Children of HIV-positive mothers** receive the same schedule with one exception: **BCG should be deferred** in known-symptomatic HIV-positive infants — confirm with facility clinician.

## Section 4 — Pre-vaccination screening

Before any vaccine, the nurse confirms:

1. Child is generally well — no high fever (>38.5°C), no severe illness in progress.
2. No anaphylaxis to a previous dose of the same vaccine.
3. Child is at the correct age window for the dose.
4. Caregiver consent.

**Mild illness (low-grade fever, mild URI) is NOT a contraindication.** Many vaccinations are missed unnecessarily because of minor illness. Counsel caregivers that mild illness doesn't preclude vaccination.

## Section 5 — Catch-up immunization

If a child has missed a dose:

1. **Don't restart the series.** Continue from the dose missed.
2. **Maintain minimum intervals** between doses of multi-dose antigens (typically 4 weeks for primary series).
3. **Catch-up window**: most antigens can be given up to age 5 or beyond. Confirm with EPI coordinator for upper age limits.
4. **Document each dose given** on the child's growth card.

CHW role in catch-up:

- Identify under-immunized children during home visits or community outreach
- Confirm their immunization status against their growth card
- Refer to facility for catch-up doses
- Follow up to confirm doses were given

## Section 6 — Counseling caregivers

Common caregiver concerns and responses:

| Concern | Response |
|---|---|
| "My child got a fever after the last vaccine" | Mild fever (< 38.5°C) is common after pentavalent and is normal. Manage with paracetamol per facility guidance. Continue the schedule. |
| "I heard vaccines cause autism" | This claim is not supported by evidence. The original study has been retracted. Vaccines are extensively safety-tested. Direct to district health team for community-level conversation if widespread. |
| "My child is sick today" | Mild illness is not a reason to skip. Severe illness — defer briefly and reschedule. |
| "We're traveling, can we delay?" | Try to maintain the schedule. If delay is unavoidable, catch up as soon as possible. Don't restart. |
| "It hurts the baby" | Brief discomfort is normal. The protection is significantly greater than the discomfort. Hold the baby, breastfeed during the injection, comfort afterward. |

## Section 7 — Cold chain awareness (CHW context)

CHWs do not transport vaccines (that's the facility's role), but should know:

- Vaccines must stay at 2–8°C until administered.
- A vaccine that has been frozen is potentially damaged and must NOT be used.
- A vaccine that has been at room temperature for too long may be ineffective.
- If a CHW observes any cold-chain breach at the facility (broken fridge, missing ice packs in carry boxes), report to the EPI coordinator.

## Section 8 — Vaccine-preventable disease outbreaks

CHWs may be the first to notice a cluster of a vaccine-preventable disease in their community. Report any of the following immediately:

- Cluster of children with rash + fever (suspected measles)
- Acute flaccid paralysis in any child (potential polio — extremely rare, but every case is investigated)
- Suspected pertussis (whooping cough) in unvaccinated infants
- Cluster of neonatal tetanus

These reports drive supplementary immunization campaigns.

## Section 9 — When to escalate

Escalate immediately to the **District EPI Coordinator** when:

- Cluster of vaccine-preventable disease cases (measles, suspected pertussis, AFP)
- Stockout of any antigen at facility for more than 48 hours
- Cold-chain failure at facility (broken fridge, prolonged power outage)
- Caregiver refusing all immunization for a child despite counseling — district team can engage at community level
- Suspected vaccine-related severe adverse event (anaphylaxis, persistent high fever)
- Catch-up needed for a child older than the standard upper age limits

## Section 10 — Data and reporting

Immunization indicators reported monthly:

- Number of doses administered, by antigen
- Number of children fully immunized by 12 months
- Drop-out rate (e.g., Pentavalent 1 → Pentavalent 3 drop-out)
- Number of children identified for catch-up
- Number of cold-chain incidents
- Number of suspected vaccine-preventable disease cases reported

These data feed the quarterly EPI review at MoH and inform supplementary immunization activity planning.
