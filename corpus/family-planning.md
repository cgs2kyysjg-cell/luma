# Family Planning & Contraception Counseling

## Section 1 — Definitions

- **Family planning (FP)**: enabling individuals and couples to decide whether and when to have children, and to have the means to act on that decision.
- **Contraceptive method**: a tool, drug, or behavior used to prevent pregnancy.
- **LARC (Long-Acting Reversible Contraception)**: methods effective for ≥ 3 years without daily action — implants and IUDs.
- **Dual protection**: simultaneous protection from pregnancy and STIs/HIV. Typically achieved by using a condom in combination with another method.
- **Unmet need**: a person who wants to delay or avoid pregnancy but is not using a contraceptive method. Reducing unmet need is a core programme goal.

## Section 2 — Roles and responsibilities

- **CHW**: counsels on method options at home visits and community sessions; distributes condoms; refers to facility for clinical methods (implants, IUDs, injectables); provides oral contraceptive resupply where trained and authorized.
- **Facility nurse / midwife**: counsels on method choice; inserts and removes implants and IUDs; administers injectables; manages side effects and complications.
- **District FP coordinator**: ensures method supply, monitors uptake.

CHWs in many SSA programmes including Lesotho are authorized to provide select methods (condoms, oral contraceptives, injectables in some contexts) at the community level. Confirm the current scope of CHW authorization with the District FP Coordinator.

## Section 4 — Counseling approach

The WHO-recommended counseling structure (often abbreviated GATHER):

- **G**reet
- **A**sk about reproductive intentions and history
- **T**ell about the available methods
- **H**elp the client choose
- **E**xplain how to use the chosen method
- **R**eturn for follow-up

Key counseling principles:

1. **Client choice is paramount.** Provide accurate information about all methods; do not steer toward a specific one based on programme targets.
2. **No coercion.** Tubal ligation, IUD, and implant are reversible (well, all except sterilization) but the client must choose freely without pressure.
3. **Discuss side effects honestly** — every method has trade-offs. Pretending side effects don't exist undermines trust and adherence.
4. **Discuss STI/HIV protection separately.** Condoms are the only method that prevents STIs and HIV. If pregnancy prevention is the primary goal but HIV/STI risk exists, recommend dual protection.
5. **Adolescent-friendly counseling**: confidentiality, no parental notification requirement (in most jurisdictions; confirm Lesotho-specific age of consent for FP).

## Section 5 — Method-specific guidance

### 5.1 Condoms (CHW-deliverable)

- Distribute openly; do not require justification.
- Show how to use correctly: open carefully (not with teeth), check expiration, ensure correct orientation, leave a small space at the tip, use water-based lubricant only, dispose after one use.
- Stock should be discreet for clients who request it.
- Remind clients: condoms work for both pregnancy and HIV/STI prevention.

### 5.2 Oral contraceptive pills (where CHW-authorized)

- Combined oral contraceptive (COC) requires a brief eligibility check: age, smoking status, blood pressure history, breastfeeding status. WHO Medical Eligibility Criteria (MEC) wheel is the standard reference.
- Progestin-only pill (POP) is suitable for breastfeeding women and clients with COC contraindications.
- Counsel on consistent daily use at the same time. Missed pills have specific catch-up rules per the MEC.
- Side effects in first 2–3 months (nausea, breakthrough bleeding) are common and usually resolve.

### 5.3 Injectables (where CHW-authorized)

- DMPA (depot medroxyprogesterone acetate) is given every 3 months.
- DMPA-SC (subcutaneous) can be self-injected by the client after training, increasing autonomy.
- Side effects: irregular bleeding, especially in the first cycles. May lead to amenorrhea after a year — this is normal, not a sign of damage.
- Return-to-fertility delay: average 9–10 months after the last injection. Counsel on this if the client may want to conceive soon.

### 5.4 Implants (facility-only)

- Sub-dermal rod inserted in the upper arm. 3 or 5 years depending on type.
- Local anesthetic; brief procedure.
- Side effects: irregular bleeding common, especially in the first 3 months.
- Removable at any time on client request — fertility returns immediately.

### 5.5 IUDs (facility-only)

- Copper IUD: hormone-free, up to 12 years.
- Hormonal IUD: contains progestin, 5–8 years, often reduces menstrual bleeding.
- Insertion is a brief facility procedure; rare complications.
- Removable at any time; fertility returns immediately.

### 5.6 Lactational amenorrhea (LAM)

A natural method effective in the first 6 months postpartum IF all three criteria are met:

1. The mother is exclusively breastfeeding (no other food or drink for the baby)
2. The mother has not had a menstrual period since giving birth
3. The baby is less than 6 months old

If any criterion fails, LAM is no longer reliable and another method is needed.

## Section 6 — Postpartum family planning

A key window for family planning is the postpartum period. CHW conducts counseling at:

- 6-week postnatal visit (the standard FP counseling moment)
- Discharge from delivery (if facility delivery)

Method options postpartum:

- LAM if criteria met (see § 5.6)
- Progestin-only pill from week 6 (safe with breastfeeding)
- DMPA from week 6
- IUD: can be inserted immediately post-delivery or at 6-week visit
- Implant: can be inserted at any time postpartum
- Avoid combined oral contraceptive for first 6 months while breastfeeding (estrogen reduces milk supply)

Pregnancies occurring within 12 months of a previous birth carry higher maternal and infant health risks — birth spacing of 24+ months is recommended.

## Section 7 — Special situations

### 7.1 Adolescents

All methods are medically eligible for adolescents who can use them correctly. Counseling should be confidential, age-appropriate, and non-judgmental. Long-acting methods (implants, IUDs) are particularly suitable for adolescents who may struggle with daily adherence.

### 7.2 HIV-positive clients on ART

Most contraceptive methods are compatible with ART. A few drug interactions exist (specific ART combinations may reduce hormonal contraceptive effectiveness) — confirm with facility clinician. Condoms are recommended additionally for HIV transmission prevention if partner is negative.

### 7.3 Survivors of GBV

Family planning counseling for GBV survivors must include emergency contraception offer (within 72 hours of unwanted intercourse), HIV PEP eligibility (within 72 hours), and STI screening. Refer to district safe-house or GBV services. Family planning method choice should be one the client controls without partner awareness if needed (e.g., DMPA injection is not visible to a partner; pills can be detected; implant can be detected at insertion site).

## Section 8 — When to escalate

Escalate immediately to the **District FP Coordinator** when:

- Suspected GBV during a family planning encounter
- Client requests sterilization (tubal ligation or vasectomy) — refer to facility for the proper informed-consent and surgical pathway
- Stockout of a method for more than 48 hours
- Adverse reaction to any method (severe headache, vision changes, leg pain, abdominal pain, abnormal bleeding pattern)
- Adolescent client where confidentiality concerns prevent safe access — district programme may have alternate channels

## Section 9 — Data and reporting

Family planning indicators reported monthly:

- Number of new acceptors by method
- Number of continuing users by method
- Number of method discontinuations and reasons
- Couple-years protection (CYP) by method
- Stockout days for each method

These data feed the quarterly FP review at MoH.
