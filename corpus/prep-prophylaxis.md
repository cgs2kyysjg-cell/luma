# PrEP — Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis for HIV

## Section 1 — Definitions

- **PrEP (Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis)**: daily oral antiretroviral medication taken by HIV-negative people at substantial risk of HIV exposure to prevent acquisition. The standard regimen is tenofovir disoproxil fumarate + emtricitabine (TDF/FTC), one tablet once daily.
- **Substantial risk**: a probability of HIV acquisition exceeding ~3% per year, based on individual risk profile.
- **PrEP eligibility**: anyone HIV-negative who reports ongoing exposure risk and is willing to take daily medication. PrEP is NOT a substitute for condoms or for partner ART — it's an additional tool.
- **PrEP discontinuation**: PrEP can be safely stopped when the period of substantial risk ends. Standard guidance: continue PrEP for 28 days after the last potential exposure, then stop.
- **Event-driven (2-1-1) PrEP**: an alternative dosing regimen for cisgender MSM (not currently recommended for women due to pharmacokinetic differences). 2 doses 2–24 hours before sex, 1 dose 24 hours after the first 2, 1 dose 48 hours after.

## Section 2 — Roles and responsibilities

- **CHW**: identifies people in the community who may be at substantial HIV risk (general counseling, partner notification follow-ups); refers to facility for PrEP assessment.
- **Facility nurse**: HIV testing (must be negative); PrEP eligibility assessment; baseline labs (creatinine, hepatitis B); prescription; quarterly follow-up.
- **District HIV programme coordinator**: ensures PrEP supply, monitors uptake.

CHWs do not prescribe PrEP. They support identification, linkage, and adherence.

## Section 3 — Who should be offered PrEP

PrEP is offered to people HIV-negative who have substantial risk. Specific populations to actively offer PrEP include:

1. **Sero-discordant partner of an HIV-positive person** whose partner is not yet virally suppressed, or has been suppressed for less than 6 months
2. **Sex workers** (any gender)
3. **Gay men and other men who have sex with men** (MSM)
4. **Transgender people** with substantial exposure risk
5. **People who inject drugs** with shared injection equipment exposure
6. **Pregnant or breastfeeding women** with HIV-positive partners not yet on suppressive ART, or with multiple partners
7. **Adolescent girls and young women** (15–24) in high-prevalence settings — Lesotho qualifies — with risk markers (multiple partners, transactional sex, partner of unknown status)
8. **Anyone presenting with a recent STI** — STI presentation is a marker of HIV risk
9. **Anyone after PEP** (post-exposure prophylaxis) who has ongoing risk
10. **Anyone who self-identifies risk and requests PrEP**

The list is not exhaustive. PrEP eligibility is ultimately a clinical and personal-choice decision, not an administrative gatekeeping decision.

## Section 4 — Eligibility assessment (facility)

Before starting PrEP, the facility nurse confirms:

1. **HIV status is negative**: rapid test must be non-reactive within 7 days of starting PrEP. If reactive, the client is HIV-positive and should be linked to ART, NOT given PrEP.
2. **No symptoms of acute HIV**: fever, lymph node enlargement, rash within 4 weeks of suspected recent exposure may indicate window-period acute HIV. Defer PrEP, repeat test in 4 weeks.
3. **Hepatitis B status known**: if hepatitis B-positive, abrupt PrEP discontinuation can cause hepatitis flare. Counsel on this risk.
4. **Renal function**: baseline creatinine. PrEP is generally safe but creatinine should be checked at baseline and at 3-month and 12-month follow-ups.
5. **Pregnancy status**: PrEP is safe in pregnancy. No exclusion.
6. **Willingness to take daily medication**: clients should understand PrEP requires daily adherence to be effective.

## Section 5 — Initiation

- Initial prescription: typically 30 days, with the next refill at 1-month follow-up.
- Counsel on time to protection: oral PrEP takes ~7 days of daily dosing to reach protective levels for receptive anal sex, ~21 days for receptive vaginal sex.
- Counsel on side effects: nausea or headache common in first 2 weeks; usually resolves. Severe rash, jaundice, or persistent vomiting → return to facility.
- Provide condoms alongside PrEP. PrEP does not protect against other STIs or pregnancy.
- Set the next visit date: 1 month from start.

## Section 6 — Follow-up schedule

| Visit | Timing | What |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 1 month after initiation | HIV re-test, side-effect screen, adherence check |
| 2 | 3 months | HIV re-test, creatinine, STI screen if indicated, refill (3 months) |
| 3 | 6 months | HIV re-test, creatinine, refill |
| Quarterly thereafter | every 3 months | HIV re-test, creatinine, refill |

If at any visit the HIV test is reactive → discontinue PrEP, refer for confirmation and ART initiation immediately.

## Section 7 — Discontinuation

PrEP can be discontinued when:

- The client is no longer at substantial risk (relationship status change, behavior change, partner now virally suppressed for ≥ 6 months)
- The client requests to stop
- The client cannot tolerate side effects
- The client has confirmed HIV-positive (switch to ART)
- The client has acute renal injury

When discontinuing electively, advise continuing for **28 days after the last potential exposure**, then stop. Do not stop the same day as last exposure.

## Section 8 — Special situations

### 8.1 Adolescent girls and young women

Confidentiality and discretion are critical. Many adolescents will not start or continue PrEP if family awareness is a concern. Offer in private, age-appropriate settings.

### 8.2 Pregnant and breastfeeding women

PrEP is safe and recommended for pregnant or breastfeeding women at substantial risk. Vertical transmission risk is real if the woman seroconverts during pregnancy or breastfeeding — PrEP prevents that.

### 8.3 People who inject drugs

PrEP is part of a harm-reduction package alongside needle-syringe programmes, opioid agonist therapy where available, and safe-injection counseling. Refer to specialized harm-reduction services where available.

### 8.4 Post-PEP transition

Anyone completing a course of PEP (post-exposure prophylaxis) who has ongoing risk should be transitioned directly onto PrEP without an interruption. Counsel on the difference: PEP for after-the-fact exposure (28 days), PrEP for ongoing exposure prevention.

## Section 9 — When to escalate

Escalate immediately to the **District HIV Programme Coordinator** when:

- Client on PrEP tests HIV-positive at any quarterly visit — confirmation and ART initiation required
- Acute renal injury on baseline or follow-up creatinine
- Stockout of PrEP at facility for more than 48 hours
- Client requests PrEP for a child under 15 — discuss case-by-case with district coordinator
- Suspected GBV during PrEP eligibility assessment — link to safe-house services first

## Section 10 — Data and reporting

PrEP indicators reported monthly:

- Number of clients newly initiated on PrEP (by population: AGYW, MSM, sex worker, sero-discordant, etc.)
- Number continuing PrEP at month 1 / month 3 / month 6
- Number of HIV seroconversions during PrEP use
- Number of PrEP discontinuations (by reason)

These data feed the quarterly HIV programme review at MoH.
