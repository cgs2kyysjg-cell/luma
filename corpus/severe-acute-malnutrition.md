# Severe Acute Malnutrition (SAM) Management

## Section 1 — Definitions

- **Acute malnutrition**: a recent loss of body weight from inadequate food intake or illness. Reversible with proper nutrition support.
- **Moderate Acute Malnutrition (MAM)**: weight-for-height between -2 and -3 standard deviations (SD), OR mid-upper arm circumference (MUAC) 115–125 mm, without bilateral pitting edema.
- **Severe Acute Malnutrition (SAM)**: weight-for-height < -3 SD, OR MUAC < 115 mm, OR bilateral pitting edema (regardless of weight). Three independent criteria — any one alone triggers SAM diagnosis.
- **Complicated SAM**: SAM with any medical complication (severe dehydration, severe pneumonia, no appetite, hypothermia, severe edema, lethargy). Requires inpatient stabilization.
- **Uncomplicated SAM**: SAM with appetite preserved, no complications, child alert. Managed at outpatient level via CMAM.
- **RUTF (Ready-to-Use Therapeutic Food)**: a calorie-dense paste-like food (e.g., Plumpy'Nut) that doesn't require water or refrigeration. The mainstay of outpatient SAM treatment.

## Section 2 — Roles and responsibilities

- **CHW**: identifies children with SAM via MUAC measurement and edema check during home visits and growth-monitoring sessions; refers SAM cases to facility; supports caregivers through outpatient CMAM treatment.
- **Facility nurse**: confirms SAM diagnosis; assesses for complications; initiates outpatient or inpatient pathway; manages weekly RUTF distribution.
- **Inpatient stabilization centre**: manages complicated SAM and the stabilization phase before transition to outpatient care.
- **District nutrition coordinator**: RUTF supply, programme oversight.

CHWs do NOT prescribe RUTF doses or manage inpatient stabilization. They identify, refer, and support adherence.

## Section 3 — Identification: MUAC and edema

The two simplest field assessments for SAM, both within CHW capability:

### 3.1 MUAC measurement

For children **6–59 months**:

1. Have the child stand or sit comfortably; left arm relaxed at the side.
2. Locate the midpoint between the shoulder bone and the elbow.
3. Wrap the MUAC tape around the arm at the midpoint, snug but not constricting.
4. Read the colored zone on the tape:
   - **Green** (≥ 125 mm): normal — no acute malnutrition by MUAC.
   - **Yellow** (115–124 mm): MAM — refer for outpatient nutrition support.
   - **Red** (< 115 mm): SAM — refer same-day to facility.

### 3.2 Edema check

Press both feet firmly with thumbs for 3 seconds. If a pit (depression) remains visible after release, that is bilateral pitting edema. Edema indicates SAM regardless of MUAC reading.

Edema severity:

- Grade +: feet only
- Grade ++: feet and lower legs
- Grade +++: generalized (face, arms, body) — emergency referral

Any grade of bilateral pitting edema = SAM = same-day facility referral.

### 3.3 Visible severe wasting

A child who looks visibly severely wasted (very thin, ribs visible, "old man" face) should be assessed even if MUAC is in the yellow zone. Clinical judgment matters; the cut-offs miss some severe cases.

## Section 4 — Triage at facility

When a CHW refers a child for SAM, the facility nurse:

1. Confirms MUAC and edema.
2. Performs the **appetite test**: offer the child RUTF; if the child eats voluntarily, appetite is preserved. If the child refuses or vomits, appetite is failed.
3. Performs the **complications screen**: any of the following = complicated SAM = inpatient referral:
   - Bilateral pitting edema grade +++
   - Failed appetite test
   - Severe dehydration (sunken eyes, lethargic, slow skin pinch return)
   - Hypothermia (axillary temperature < 35.0°C)
   - Severe pneumonia (chest indrawing, fast breathing, stridor)
   - Severe anemia (palmar pallor)
   - Lethargy or unconsciousness
   - Persistent vomiting
4. Triage outcome:
   - **Uncomplicated SAM** → outpatient CMAM with weekly facility follow-up
   - **Complicated SAM** → inpatient stabilization centre

## Section 5 — Outpatient CMAM (uncomplicated SAM)

Children with uncomplicated SAM are managed at home with weekly facility visits:

1. **RUTF prescription** by the facility nurse; weekly supply provided.
2. **CHW home visits**: confirm RUTF is being eaten by the malnourished child only (not shared with siblings — common cause of programme failure).
3. **Weekly weight and MUAC re-check** at facility. Document on the CMAM card.
4. **Routine medications** at admission: vitamin A, deworming (after 2 weeks of RUTF), measles vaccine if not yet given.
5. **Discharge criteria**: MUAC ≥ 125 mm AND no edema for 2 consecutive weekly visits, OR weight-for-height ≥ -2 SD for 2 visits, AND minimum stay of 8 weeks.

CHW counsel for caregivers:

- "Give the RUTF to the malnourished child, not the others. The siblings are not malnourished — they don't need it."
- "Open one packet at a time. Don't mix with porridge or water — give it as it comes."
- "Continue breastfeeding if still applicable."
- "Bring the child for the weekly weigh-in even if they look better — we need to confirm they keep gaining."

## Section 6 — Inpatient stabilization (complicated SAM)

Children with complicated SAM are admitted to a stabilization centre for the initial phase:

- F-75 therapeutic milk (low-calorie, electrolyte-balanced) for the first days
- Treatment of complications (rehydration, antibiotics, etc.)
- Transition to F-100 or RUTF as appetite returns
- Discharge to outpatient CMAM once complications resolve and appetite returns

CHWs do not manage inpatient care but support follow-up after discharge.

## Section 7 — Underlying causes

SAM is rarely just a feeding issue. Look for and address underlying contributors:

1. **Acute illness** (TB, HIV, severe diarrhea, persistent infection)
2. **Food insecurity** at the household level — link to social support / food assistance
3. **Maternal/caregiver illness** affecting feeding (maternal depression, maternal HIV, maternal substance use)
4. **Caregiver knowledge gaps** about feeding (e.g., excessive water in porridge diluting nutrition)
5. **Poor sanitation** leading to repeated diarrheal episodes
6. **Adolescent or first-time mother** with limited support

CHW identifies these contributors during home visits and links to appropriate services.

## Section 8 — Special situations

### 8.1 HIV-positive children with SAM

HIV co-infection is common in pediatric SAM in high-prevalence settings. Every child with SAM should be HIV-tested if status is unknown. HIV-positive children with SAM need both ART and SAM treatment in parallel — recovery is slower.

### 8.2 TB co-infection

Persistent failure to gain weight despite adequate RUTF intake is a TB warning sign. Refer for TB workup.

### 8.3 Infants under 6 months with SAM

Infants under 6 months are NOT given RUTF. They need facility-based assessment, often inpatient management focused on supporting breastfeeding (maternal nutrition, breastfeeding technique, supplementary feeds where indicated). Refer all SAM-suspect infants under 6 months to facility same-day.

### 8.4 Cluster of SAM cases in one village

If the CHW identifies 3 or more SAM cases in one village or household cluster, escalate to district nutrition coordinator. May indicate a household-level food security crisis or programme failure that needs broader response.

## Section 9 — When to escalate

Escalate immediately to the **District Nutrition Coordinator** when:

- Cluster of 3+ SAM cases in one village within 1 month
- Stockout of RUTF at facility for more than 48 hours
- Child in outpatient CMAM losing weight after 3 consecutive weekly visits
- Child meeting any complicated-SAM criteria observed during home visit (lethargy, no feeding, severe edema)
- Maternal death in a household with a malnourished surviving child

## Section 10 — Data and reporting

SAM programme indicators reported monthly:

- Number of children screened (MUAC + edema check)
- Number of new SAM cases identified
- Number admitted to outpatient CMAM
- Number admitted to inpatient stabilization
- Discharge outcomes: cured, defaulted, died, non-recovered, transferred
- Mean length of stay in CMAM
- RUTF stockout days

These data feed the quarterly nutrition programme review at MoH.
