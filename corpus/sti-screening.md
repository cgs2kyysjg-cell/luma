# STI Screening & Syndromic Management

## Section 1 — Definitions

- **STI (Sexually Transmitted Infection)**: an infection transmitted primarily through sexual contact. Common STIs include chlamydia, gonorrhea, syphilis, trichomoniasis, genital herpes, HPV, hepatitis B, and HIV.
- **Syndromic management**: managing a patient based on the cluster of clinical signs/symptoms (the "syndrome") without waiting for laboratory confirmation. Used at primary care level where lab access is limited. Trades some specificity for speed and access.
- **Asymptomatic STI**: many STIs (especially chlamydia in women, all STIs in early stages) cause no symptoms. Asymptomatic infections still transmit and still cause complications. Screening of asymptomatic high-risk groups is standard.
- **Partner notification**: informing the sexual partners of an STI-positive client so they can be tested and treated. Critical for breaking transmission cycles.
- **PID (Pelvic Inflammatory Disease)**: complication of untreated chlamydia or gonorrhea in women. Causes infertility, ectopic pregnancy, chronic pelvic pain.

## Section 2 — Roles and responsibilities

- **CHW**: identifies clients reporting STI symptoms during home visits or community sessions; refers to facility for syndromic management; provides condom counseling; supports partner notification follow-up.
- **Facility nurse / clinician**: takes sexual history, performs syndromic assessment, provides treatment per algorithm, performs HIV and syphilis testing, manages follow-up.
- **District STI focal person**: oversight, drug supply, programme indicators.

CHWs do not prescribe STI treatment. They support identification, linkage, condom distribution, and partner notification.

## Section 3 — Common STI syndromes (overview)

WHO syndromic management identifies four primary syndromes treated at primary care level:

| Syndrome | Common in | Likely pathogens |
|---|---|---|
| Urethral discharge | Men | Gonorrhea, chlamydia |
| Vaginal discharge | Women | Chlamydia, gonorrhea, trichomoniasis, bacterial vaginosis, candidiasis |
| Lower abdominal pain | Women | PID (chlamydia, gonorrhea); ectopic pregnancy must be excluded |
| Genital ulcer | Both | Syphilis, herpes, chancroid |

Plus:

- **Inguinal bubo**: enlarged lymph nodes in the groin — chancroid or LGV
- **Scrotal swelling**: epididymitis from chlamydia or gonorrhea
- **Anorectal symptoms**: in MSM particularly, anorectal gonorrhea or chlamydia

CHWs identify which syndrome the client is reporting and refer to facility for management — they do not match symptoms to drugs.

## Section 4 — Screening (asymptomatic, routine)

Routine STI screening offered to:

1. **All pregnant women** at first ANC: HIV, syphilis, hepatitis B (where available)
2. **All clients presenting for HIV testing** — STI symptom screen
3. **All clients on PrEP** — quarterly STI screen
4. **All sex workers** — at every contact
5. **Sero-discordant couples** — at routine intervals
6. **Adolescents** — when risk markers present
7. **Anyone with new partner or multiple partners** — opportunistic screening

A simple symptom screen at facility or in CHW conversations:

- "Any unusual discharge, pain, or sores?"
- "Any pain during urination?"
- "Any pain during sex?"
- "Any partner with an STI diagnosis recently?"
- "Any new partner in the last 3 months?"

A 'yes' to any prompts referral for full assessment.

## Section 5 — Counseling and prevention

Standard counseling on every STI encounter:

1. **Condom use**: consistent and correct use prevents most STIs and HIV. Demonstrate use; provide a supply.
2. **Mutual monogamy or partner reduction**: discuss without judgment.
3. **STI testing for partners**: any STI diagnosis means partners need to be tested and (per syndromic management) treated.
4. **Abstinence until treatment is complete**: abstain from sex for 7 days after starting treatment AND until partners have completed treatment, to prevent reinfection.
5. **HIV testing**: every STI presentation is an entry point for HIV testing per HTS protocol.
6. **PrEP eligibility**: clients with recurring STIs are at substantial HIV risk. Offer PrEP per PrEP protocol.

## Section 6 — Partner notification

Critical for breaking transmission cycles. Three approaches (parallel to HIV index testing):

1. **Client referral**: client tells partners directly and brings them for treatment.
2. **Provider referral**: with consent, the health worker contacts named partners (typically anonymously: "you may have been exposed to an STI; please come for testing").
3. **Contract referral**: client agrees to bring partners by a deadline; if not, provider contacts.

Screen for IPV before any partner notification. Some STI diagnoses can trigger violence in domestic settings — never proceed with partner notification if IPV risk is identified.

CHW role:

- Reinforce the importance of partner notification at every contact
- Offer to support the partner notification conversation if the client is uncertain
- Track whether partners have been treated

## Section 7 — Specific STI considerations

### 7.1 Syphilis

Syphilis can be detected with rapid antibody tests at facility. Treatment is benzathine penicillin injection (one dose for early syphilis, three weekly doses for late). Pregnant women with positive syphilis must be treated immediately — congenital syphilis causes stillbirth and severe infant disease.

### 7.2 Genital herpes

Caused by HSV-1 or HSV-2. Recurrent painful lesions. Not curable but suppressible with antiviral therapy. Counseling: lifelong condition, transmission can occur even when no lesions are visible, particular care during pregnancy (active lesions at delivery may require cesarean).

### 7.3 HPV and cervical cancer

HPV (human papillomavirus) is the cause of cervical cancer. Cervical screening (Pap smear or HPV testing) every 3–5 years for women aged 30–49 is the standard prevention. HPV vaccination for adolescent girls (where introduced) prevents most high-risk strains. Lesotho HPV programme status — confirm with district programme.

### 7.4 Hepatitis B

Sexually transmitted but also vertically transmitted (mother to child). All pregnant women screened where possible. Hep B vaccine for newborns at birth (see Childhood Immunization).

## Section 8 — When to escalate

Escalate immediately to the **District STI Focal Person** when:

- Pregnant woman with positive syphilis test — must be treated within 48 hours
- Suspected PID with severe abdominal pain or fever — facility/inpatient management
- Suspected ectopic pregnancy in any woman with abdominal pain plus missed period — emergency referral
- Cluster of STI cases in a school or community group
- Suspected sexual assault or rape — link to GBV pathway, PEP within 72 hours, emergency contraception within 72 hours
- Genital ulcer not responding to first-line treatment after 7 days
- Stockout of first-line STI drugs at facility for more than 48 hours

## Section 9 — Data and reporting

STI programme indicators reported monthly:

- Number of clients presenting with each syndrome
- Number treated under syndromic management
- Number of pregnant women screened for syphilis
- Number of pregnant women treated for syphilis
- Partner notification: named, contacted, treated
- Number linked to HIV testing from STI presentation

These data feed the quarterly STI/HIV programme review at MoH.
