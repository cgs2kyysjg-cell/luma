# TB Treatment & Case Management Protocol

## Section 1 — Definitions and case categories

- **Presumptive TB**: a person with symptoms suggestive of TB (cough ≥ 2 weeks, fever, night sweats, weight loss).
- **Bacteriologically confirmed TB**: positive sputum smear, culture, or molecular test (e.g. GeneXpert MTB/RIF).
- **Clinically diagnosed TB**: chest imaging or clinical signs consistent with TB without bacteriological confirmation.
- **Drug-susceptible TB (DS-TB)**: TB confirmed susceptible to first-line agents (rifampicin and isoniazid).
- **Rifampicin-resistant TB (RR-TB) / MDR-TB**: TB resistant to rifampicin (with or without isoniazid resistance).

## Section 2 — Roles and responsibilities

- **CHW**: case finding (symptom screening), specimen referral, monthly DOT (directly observed therapy) follow-up where applicable.
- **Facility TB nurse**: clinical assessment, specimen collection, treatment initiation, monthly clinical review.
- **District TB coordinator**: oversight of all active cases in district, contact tracing supervision, drug supply.
- **National TB Programme**: drug procurement, MDR-TB treatment authorization, surveillance.

## Section 3 — Case-finding pathway

### 3.1 Symptom screening

Any patient presenting with cough lasting **≥ 2 weeks** should be screened. Additional symptoms:

1. Unexplained weight loss
2. Persistent fever (especially evening fever)
3. Night sweats
4. Hemoptysis (blood in sputum)
5. Loss of appetite

**HIV-positive patients** should be screened at every clinical encounter regardless of symptom duration — TB risk is significantly elevated.

### 3.2 Specimen collection

For any presumptive case:

1. Collect two sputum samples (spot-and-morning, OR two spots one hour apart).
2. Send to laboratory for GeneXpert MTB/RIF testing where available; smear microscopy as fallback.
3. Document on the TB suspect register at the facility.

### 3.3 Result interpretation

- **GeneXpert positive, rifampicin-susceptible**: initiate first-line treatment (Section 4).
- **GeneXpert positive, rifampicin-resistant**: refer to district TB coordinator for MDR-TB pathway (Section 5). Do NOT initiate first-line treatment.
- **GeneXpert negative but high clinical suspicion**: refer for chest X-ray and clinical assessment.

## Section 4 — Drug-susceptible TB treatment

### 4.1 Standard regimen

The standard first-line regimen for new adult DS-TB cases is:

- **Intensive phase (2 months)**: 4-drug fixed-dose combination (HRZE) — isoniazid, rifampicin, pyrazinamide, ethambutol.
- **Continuation phase (4 months)**: 2-drug combination (HR) — isoniazid, rifampicin.

Total treatment duration: **6 months**.

Dosing follows the WHO weight-band tables. **CHWs do not prescribe or adjust dosing.** Dose adjustments are made by the facility nurse or clinician using the weight-band reference card.

### 4.2 Treatment initiation

Before starting treatment, the facility nurse must:

1. Confirm bacteriological or clinical diagnosis.
2. Test for HIV co-infection (or confirm prior status).
3. Counsel the patient on the 6-month commitment, side effects, and DOT requirements.
4. Register the case in the TB treatment register and assign a unique TB case number.
5. Initiate treatment same-day where possible.

### 4.3 DOT and monthly follow-up

CHW conducts directly observed therapy where feasible, especially in the intensive phase. Minimum monthly follow-up by CHW or facility staff to confirm:

1. Treatment adherence (pill count or DOT log)
2. Symptom improvement
3. Side effects (nausea, jaundice, peripheral neuropathy, vision changes)
4. Weight (re-dose at month 2 if significant change)

### 4.4 Monitoring smears

Sputum smear (or GeneXpert) is repeated:

- **End of month 2** (end of intensive phase): if positive, extend intensive phase by 1 month and refer for clinical review. Possible drug-susceptibility re-test indicated.
- **End of month 5**: if positive, treatment failure declared; refer for MDR-TB workup.
- **End of month 6** (treatment completion): final smear required to declare cure.

## Section 5 — Drug-resistant TB pathway

### 5.1 When to escalate

Any patient with:

- Rifampicin resistance on GeneXpert
- Treatment failure (positive smear at month 5)
- Recurrence within 12 months of completing prior treatment
- Known contact with a confirmed MDR-TB case

… is referred immediately to the district TB coordinator for MDR-TB workup. **First-line treatment is not initiated for these patients.**

### 5.2 MDR-TB regimen authorization

MDR-TB regimens (including bedaquiline-containing shorter or longer regimens) are initiated **only by authorized MDR-TB clinicians** at designated facilities. CHWs and primary-care nurses do not initiate or adjust these regimens.

If a patient on MDR-TB treatment presents to the facility with adverse effects or adherence issues, document and refer to the MDR-TB clinician same-day.

### 5.3 Adherence support for MDR-TB patients

MDR-TB treatment is significantly longer (9–18+ months) and harder to tolerate. CHW role:

1. Daily contact during the first 2 months where feasible.
2. Watch for QT prolongation symptoms (palpitations, fainting) — refer immediately.
3. Watch for hearing loss (older injectable regimens) — refer immediately.
4. Psychosocial support is essential; link to peer support where available.

## Section 6 — Contact tracing

For every confirmed TB case:

1. Identify household and close contacts (anyone sleeping in the same dwelling for ≥ 1 week in the prior 3 months, or significant occupational contact).
2. Screen each contact for symptoms within 7 days of index case diagnosis.
3. Refer symptomatic contacts for full TB workup.
4. **Children under 5 in contact with a confirmed case**: refer for TB preventive therapy (TPT) evaluation regardless of symptoms.
5. **HIV-positive contacts**: refer for TPT evaluation regardless of symptoms.

Contact tracing is documented on the facility contact register and reported monthly.

## Section 7 — When to escalate

Escalate immediately to the **District TB Coordinator** when:

- Rifampicin resistance is detected on GeneXpert
- Treatment failure (positive smear at month 5) is suspected
- Patient on first-line treatment develops jaundice (suspected hepatotoxicity)
- Patient on first-line treatment develops vision changes (ethambutol toxicity)
- Patient on MDR-TB treatment develops palpitations or hearing changes
- Pediatric TB case where weight-band dosing is unclear
- Active TB contact under age 5 cannot be located

## Section 8 — Data and reporting

Facility-level TB indicators reported monthly:

- Number of presumptive cases screened
- Number of bacteriologically confirmed cases
- Treatment outcomes by cohort (cured, completed, failed, died, lost to follow-up)
- Contact tracing completion rate
- Time from symptom onset to treatment initiation (median)

National TB surveillance aggregates these indicators quarterly.
